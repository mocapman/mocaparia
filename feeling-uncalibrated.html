My Perspective on Health,
Life, and the Time I Have

I want to share a bit about where I am in terms of my health, my mindset about the future, and how I’ve been thinking about things. This isn't about seeking advice or validation—it's just my way of sharing where I’m at.

I’m realistic about my health and longevity. Given my medical history—things like pancreatitis, kidney failure, diabetes, and having a pacemaker—I know that my time may be shorter than others. My family history shows a trend of early death, and as I turn 58 this year, I don’t expect to live a long, long life. I’ve come to terms with that reality, but I don’t see it as something to dwell on. It’s just the way things are, and I’ve learned to accept it without feeling like I’m resigning to fate.

I’ve spent some time thinking about what it means to live well, not just live longer. For me, this means focusing on quality of life, not necessarily the length of life. I’ve let go of trying to chase an ideal of perfect health, because I know that’s not realistic. My goal now is to enjoy my life for as long as I can and to do what I can to stay healthy enough to do the things I care about. I don’t want to spend all my energy on managing symptoms or focusing on what’s wrong—I want to find balance, and that’s something I’m still learning how to do.

Doctors have helped me immensely, and I don’t take their care for granted. But I also recognize that they can’t give me everything. They can manage my conditions, but ultimately, it’s up to me to shape my approach to living. I’ve had to accept my body’s limitations and learn how to live within them, finding ways to focus on feeling good today, rather than constantly fighting for some unattainable version of health.

What I’m really trying to find is that balance between managing symptoms and living fully. I don’t want to be fixated on my past health struggles or what the future might hold. Instead, I try to stay present in the moment, enjoy the things I can, and accept that things won’t always go perfectly.

What’s helped me in this process is recognizing how interconnected life is with the people around me. I owe my life to my own resilience, certainly, but also to the care and love of my family, the doctors who’ve worked with me, and the strength that comes from the relationships I’ve built. My wife and kids have played such a big role in helping me keep going. They’ve been a source of love and purpose, which I think has given me the strength to face my health challenges. Even now, my role as a father continues to be important to me, even though my kids are grown. They still look to me for support and advice, and I’m still here for them in whatever way I can be.

I’ve also had to redefine myself in retirement. Leaving my job was a big change, but it’s given me the space to explore other parts of life and to focus more on things that bring me joy, like writing and traveling. I don’t feel like I’m “done” with anything, just shifting focus. Retirement isn’t about stepping away from life—it’s about finding new ways to live it.

The way I look at it, life isn’t about being perfect or achieving immortality. It’s about connection, purpose, and finding meaning in the time we have. I don’t have it all figured out, but I’m learning to embrace what I can, let go of what I can’t change, and find peace in the moments I have.

I wanted to share this with you just to give some insight into where I’m at right now. This is how I’m thinking about things, and I’m okay with it. I’m not asking for anything, but I think it helps to understand where I’m coming from as I move forward in life.
